<Catalyst>
The seed extension is the load-bearing pre-billboard event. Without it, Artisan does not have the ~$13.8M cumulative capital base that made a multi-placement OOH campaign affordable in the first month after closing. The Stop Hiring Humans launch six weeks later is paid-marketing arbitrage built directly on top of this round.
</Catalyst>

September 2024. Artisan closes a seed extension to a cumulative $11.5M. Oliver Jung — the early-stage angel from the pre-seed — leads. HubSpot, Y Combinator, and Sequoia participate alongside the existing investor base. The May 2024 $7.3M seed (BOND Capital + Soma Capital) is now subsumed into the larger round.

## The capital cadence

| Round | Date | Amount | Lead | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-seed | Nov 15, 2023 | $2.3M | YC + Bayhouse + Oliver Jung | $2.3M |
| Seed | May 16, 2024 | $7.3M | BOND Capital + Soma | $9.6M |
| Seed extension | Sep 2024 | +$4.2M (to $11.5M total) | Oliver Jung | <Stat value="$13.8M" label="cumulative pre-billboard" /> |

The cadence matters. Three rounds in 10 months is fast; the lead investor on the extension being a returning angel rather than a new VC is a signal that the round was insider-led — close existing investors topping up before a marketing push, not a new VC re-pricing the company.

## What the extension funded

The September 2024 round closes roughly six weeks before the October 2024 SF billboard campaign launches. The order of operations is what makes the campaign affordable:

1. **Capital base reaches ~$13.8M cumulative.** A multi-placement OOH campaign — Highway 101 billboard near SFO + ~50 SF bus shelters + TechCrunch Disrupt main booth — runs in the $50K to $500K range for the SF window alone. With ~$13.8M raised the campaign is a single-digit percent of capital; without the extension it would be a much higher fraction.
2. **HubSpot, Sequoia, and YC participate as scout / strategic.** The signal is that the company is investable to brand-name VCs at the next stage. This is the validation that any subsequent Series A would price against — even if the lead Series A investor (Glade Brook Capital) ultimately came from outside this group.
3. **Oliver Jung as repeat lead.** Returning-angel-as-lead patterns typically reflect that no priced VC was ready to lead at the desired terms, and the existing investor base wanted to extend runway through a milestone before re-pricing. The milestone Artisan was running toward was the campaign and the press cycle that would price the Series A.

## Why the timing matters

Most companies that close a seed extension in September spend October planning a Series A road show. Artisan spent October launching a paid OOH campaign aimed at TechCrunch Disrupt. The decision to deploy capital into marketing rather than hold it for runway is what makes this round structurally interesting — and what made the Series A six months later land at <Stat value="$25M" label="Series A April 2025 (Glade Brook lead)" /> on top of a press cycle that the seed extension had funded.

The implicit calculation: a $50K–$500K OOH campaign + earned-media amplification was a higher-leverage use of capital than a Series A road show without that campaign behind it. By April 2025 — six months later — the calculation had been priced. Whether the durability of revenue acquired through the campaign justifies the calculation is the open question this case study tracks.

<Conclusion title="What to copy">

- **Time the extension to fund the press cycle that prices the next round.** Most extension rounds are runway top-ups. Artisan's was a paid-marketing budget for a campaign that priced the Series A. The order of operations matters.
- **Insider-led extensions can be a strategic choice, not a downgrade.** A returning angel as lead is sometimes interpreted as weakness. In this case it appears to have been the cleanest path to fund the marketing push without re-pricing on incomplete traction.
- **The capital base for a paid-OOH B3 catalyst is real.** A campaign of Artisan's shape requires single-digit percent of a ~$13.8M base to be affordable. Teams without that base ship into a different cost-of-attention environment.

</Conclusion>
